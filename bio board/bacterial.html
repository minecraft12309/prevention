Bacterial Diseases

These diseases are caused by bacterial pathogens, which can enter the body through various routes, such as ingestion, inhalation, or breaks in the skin.

Tuberculosis

  • Causative Organism: Mycobacterium tuberculosis

  • Transmission: Airborne; spread through inhalation of droplets from coughs, sneezes, or speech of an infected person with active TB.

  • Symptoms: Persistent cough (lasting more than 3 weeks), coughing up blood or mucus, chest pain, fever, night sweats, unexplained weight loss, fatigue, and loss of appetite.

  • Prevention: BCG vaccine (particularly for children in high-risk areas), good ventilation in living and working areas, early detection and treatment, and infection control in healthcare settings.

  • Treatment: Standard 6-9 month antibiotic regimen (isoniazid, rifampicin, ethambutol, pyrazinamide); for drug-resistant TB, second-line antibiotics over 18-24 months.

Leptospirosis

  • Causative Organism: Leptospira bacteria

  • Transmission: Spread through direct contact with water, soil, or food contaminated with the urine of infected animals (commonly rats); can enter the body through cuts in the skin, mucous membranes, or ingestion.

  • Symptoms: Fever, headache, muscle pain (especially in the calves), chills, red eyes, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, and in severe cases, jaundice, kidney damage, or meningitis.

  • Prevention: Avoid swimming or wading in potentially contaminated water, using protective clothing in high-risk areas, proper sanitation, and rodent control.

  • Treatment: Antibiotics such as doxycycline or penicillin, especially when given early; in severe cases, hospitalization and supportive care may be required.

Diphtheria

  • Causative Organism: Corynebacterium diphtheriae

  • Transmission: Spread through respiratory droplets when an infected person coughs or sneezes, or through direct contact with contaminated surfaces or objects.

  • Symptoms: Sore throat, fever, swollen lymph nodes, weakness, and the formation of a thick gray or white coating in the throat or nose (pseudomembrane), which can lead to breathing difficulties.

  • Prevention: Vaccination with the diphtheria toxoid vaccine (part of the DTaP or Tdap vaccine), good hygiene practices, and avoiding close contact with infected individuals.

  • Treatment: Antitoxin to neutralize the toxin and antibiotics (such as penicillin or erythromycin) to eliminate the bacteria; supportive care may be necessary for severe cases.
